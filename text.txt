
# Chapter 1 — Introduction and Problem Definition

## 1.1 Introduction

The rapid growth of e-commerce and on-demand delivery services has significantly increased the pressure placed on urban logistics systems worldwide. Traditional ground-based transportation networks frequently suffer from traffic congestion, infrastructure limitations, environmental constraints, and increasing operational costs. These challenges are particularly evident in rapidly developing metropolitan regions such as the Municipality of Tirana, Albania.

As the country's political, economic, and demographic center, Tirana experiences substantial daily traffic congestion, especially within the urban core. Conventional delivery vehicles operating within this environment face increasing travel times, reduced operational efficiency, higher fuel consumption, and greater environmental impact. Consequently, there is growing interest in alternative delivery methods capable of reducing dependence on congested road infrastructure.

One promising solution is the use of autonomous Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), commonly referred to as drones, for last-mile logistics operations. By utilizing low-altitude airspace instead of road infrastructure, drone-based delivery systems can potentially reduce delivery times, improve operational efficiency, and increase service accessibility in geographically challenging regions.

However, implementing such a system introduces a new set of challenges. Drone fleets are constrained by battery capacity, payload limitations, flight range restrictions, weather conditions, regulatory constraints, and infrastructure costs. Therefore, a successful deployment requires a rigorous optimization framework capable of determining:

* Where drone depots should be located.
* How many depots are required.
* How drones should be allocated among depots.
* How routes should be computed.
* How population demand should influence resource allocation.
* How operational costs can be minimized while maintaining service quality.

This project addresses these challenges through the design and implementation of TiranaFly, a population-aware drone logistics optimization platform developed using principles from Operations Research, Graph Theory, Geographic Information Systems (GIS), Artificial Intelligence, and Software Engineering.

---

## 1.2 Problem Statement

The Municipality of Tirana consists of fourteen administrative units exhibiting significant demographic heterogeneity. Population distribution is highly concentrated within the urban core, while peripheral regions display considerably lower population densities.

Such demographic asymmetry introduces important logistics planning challenges.

If resources are allocated uniformly across the municipality, densely populated areas may experience service saturation while low-density regions remain underserved. Conversely, allocating resources exclusively according to total area coverage can lead to inefficient utilization of infrastructure and fleet capacity.

Therefore, the fundamental problem investigated in this project can be formulated as follows:

"How can a drone-based logistics network be designed and optimized to provide efficient and equitable service coverage across the Municipality of Tirana while minimizing operational costs and respecting technological constraints?"

The solution requires integrating demographic demand modeling, spatial discretization, graph-theoretic routing, facility location optimization, fleet sizing methodologies, and software implementation into a unified decision-support platform.

---

The primary objective of this research is the development of a mathematically rigorous and operationally feasible drone logistics optimization framework for the Municipality of Tirana.

Specific objectives include:

2. Development of a spatial representation of Tirana using hexagonal tessellation.

3. Transformation of the spatial representation into a weighted graph suitable for routing optimization.

4. Identification of optimal depot locations using facility location methodologies.

9. Creation of an integrated software platform supporting visualization, optimization, and simulation.

10. Evaluation of system performance under multiple operational scenarios.

# 2.5 The Population Consistency Problem

During the preliminary analysis phase, a common demographic interpretation error was identified.

Several datasets report: $598,176$ as the population of Tirana.
However, this value corresponds exclusively to the administrative unit named "Tiranë" and not to the municipality as a whole.

Since the TiranaFly service area includes all fourteen administrative units, using:
$598,176$ as the denominator would produce mathematically incorrect demand weights.

The correct denominator is: $807,029$ which represents the total population of the municipality.

---

# 2.6 Mathematical Consistency Proof

Let: ${D_i}$ denote the normalized demand coefficient associated with administrative unit (i).
Demand normalization is defined as:
$${
D_i=\frac{P_i}{P_{Total}}
}$$

where:
$${
P_{Total}=807,029
}$$
By construction:

$${
\sum_{i=1}^{14} D_i
=\sum_{i=1}^{14}
\frac{P_i}{807,029}
}$$

Since:
$${
\sum_{i=1}^{14} P_i
=
807,029
}$$
we obtain:
$${\sum_{i=1}^{14} D_i=\frac{807,029}{807,029}=1}$$
This satisfies the normalization condition required for a valid demand distribution.

---

# 2.7 Consequences of Incorrect Normalization

Suppose an analyst mistakenly uses:

$${
598,176
}$$

as the denominator.

The resulting demand coefficients become:

$${
D_i^{*}=\frac{P_i}{598,176}
}$$

Summing over all administrative units:

$${\sum_{i=1}^{14}D_i^{*}=\frac{807,029}{598,176}=1.349}$$

The normalized demand would exceed 100%.

This produces several severe consequences:

### Infrastructure Distortion

Depot placement algorithms receive inflated demand values.

### Fleet Oversizing

More drones are purchased than actually required.

### Budget Inflation

Capital expenditures become artificially large.

### Simulation Errors

Demand forecasts become mathematically inconsistent.

### AI Training Bias

Machine learning models learn incorrect demand distributions.

Therefore, all calculations within TiranaFly use: ${P_{Total}=807,029}$
without exception.

---

# 2.8 Demand Modeling Philosophy

Population does not directly equal delivery demand.

However, population serves as the strongest available first-order predictor of logistics activity.

The project therefore assumes that delivery demand is proportional to population size.

Let: ${\lambda_i}$ denote the expected demand generated by administrative unit (i). Then: ${\lambda_i=\kappa P_i}$
where: $\kappa$ represents the adoption coefficient.

This coefficient captures:

* e-commerce penetration
* drone delivery adoption
* purchasing frequency
* service utilization rates

Because future adoption levels are uncertain, multiple values of $\kappa$ will be evaluated during simulation experiments.

---

# 2.9 Demand Share Model

For optimization purposes, relative demand is more important than absolute demand.

Therefore, a demand share coefficient is introduced:

$${
w_i
=
\frac{P_i}{807,029}
}$$
where:

$${
0 \leq w_i \leq 1
}$$

and:

$${
\sum_{i=1}^{14} w_i =1
}$$

The coefficient $(w_i)$ represents the proportion of total municipal demand expected to originate from administrative unit $(i)$.

These coefficients become the fundamental weights used by:

* Hexagonal tessellation generation
* Depot location optimization
* Drone allocation algorithms
* AI forecasting models
* Simulation scenarios

throughout the remainder of the project.

# Chapter 4 — Hexagonal Tessellation Framework and Spatial Discretization

---

# 4.1 Introduction

The Geographic Information System framework developed in Chapter 3 provides an accurate representation of the Municipality of Tirana through administrative boundaries and geospatial datasets.

However, geographic realism alone is insufficient for computational optimization.

Optimization algorithms generally operate more efficiently when continuous geographic space is transformed into discrete computational structures.

For this reason, the TiranaFly platform introduces a second spatial abstraction layer:

**Hexagonal Tessellation**

Instead of performing routing and optimization directly on administrative polygons, the municipality is partitioned into a collection of non-overlapping hexagonal cells.

Each hexagon becomes a computational unit capable of storing:

* Population demand
* Geographic coordinates
* Service coverage metrics
* Delivery statistics
* Graph connectivity information
* Environmental parameters

The resulting tessellation transforms the Municipality of Tirana into a structured computational environment suitable for Operations Research, Graph Theory, Artificial Intelligence, and large-scale simulation.

---

# 4.2 Spatial Discretization

Spatial discretization is the process of converting a continuous geographic region into a finite set of smaller spatial units.

Let

$${
\Omega
}$$

represent the geographic area occupied by the Municipality of Tirana.

The objective is to partition:

$${
\Omega
}$$

into:

$${
H = {h_1,h_2,\ldots,h_n}
}$$

such that:

$${
\Omega = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} h_i
}$$

while simultaneously satisfying:

$${
h_i \cap h_j = \emptyset
}$$

for all: ${i \neq j}$ this ensures complete coverage, every geographic location belongs to a cell. No Overlap, no location belongs to multiple cells.

### Computational Simplicity

Optimization algorithms operate on a finite structure.

---

# 4.3 Candidate Tessellation Structures

Several spatial discretization strategies were evaluated before selecting the final framework.

---

## 4.3.1 Square Grid

The simplest approach is a square lattice.

Advantages:

* Easy implementation
* Simple indexing
* Efficient storage

Disadvantages:

* Directional bias
* Unequal neighbor distances
* Artificial routing distortions

Consider a square cell with side length:

$${
s
}$$

Orthogonal neighbors are located at:

$${
d=s
}$$

while diagonal neighbors are located at:

$${
d=\sqrt{2}s
}$$

Thus:
$${
\sqrt{2}s \approx 1.414s
}$$

This creates anisotropic movement costs.

Routing algorithms become biased toward horizontal and vertical directions.

---

## 4.3.2 Triangular Grid

Triangular tessellations provide complete coverage.

Advantages:

* Exact tessellation
* High geometric flexibility

Disadvantages:

* Complex adjacency relationships
* Less intuitive neighborhood structure
* Higher implementation complexity

---

## 4.3.3 Voronoi Diagrams

Voronoi partitions divide space according to nearest facility distances.

Advantages:

* Natural service area modeling
* Adaptive region generation

Disadvantages:

* Irregular geometry
* Dynamic recomputation requirements
* Difficult graph construction

Voronoi diagrams are useful for service boundaries but unsuitable as the fundamental routing substrate.

---

## 4.3.4 Circular Coverage Regions

Circles provide ideal theoretical coverage.

Advantages:

* Isotropic geometry
* Minimal perimeter for fixed area

Disadvantages:

Circles cannot tessellate the plane.

Coverage inevitably creates:

* Gaps
* Overlaps

Therefore circles cannot serve as a computational grid.

---

# 4.4 Why Hexagons Were Selected

After evaluating all alternatives, regular hexagons were selected.

Hexagons satisfy every major requirement of the TiranaFly platform.

They provide:

* Complete coverage
* Uniform geometry
* Equal neighbor distances
* Efficient graph construction
* Excellent approximation of circular service areas

This combination makes hexagons the preferred spatial unit in numerous applications including:

* Cellular telecommunications
* Satellite coverage systems
* Environmental modeling
* Autonomous robotics
* Geographic information systems

---

# 4.5 Mathematical Properties of Regular Hexagons

A regular hexagon consists of six identical equilateral triangles.

Let: ${a}$ denote the side length. The area of a regular hexagon is:
$${
A_h
=
\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}a^2
}$$
The perimeter is:
$${
P_h = 6a
}$$
The circumradius equals:
$${
R=a
}$$
The inradius equals:
$${
r=
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}a
}$$
These properties provide highly regular geometric behavior.

---

# 4.6 Isotropic Neighborhood Structure

One of the strongest arguments for hexagonal grids is isotropic connectivity.

Every cell has exactly:

$${
6
}$$

adjacent neighbors.

The distance from the center of a cell to the center of any neighboring cell is identical.

Let:

$${
d_n
}$$

represent the neighbor distance.

Then:

$${
d_n = \sqrt{3}a
}$$

for all six neighboring cells.

Consequently:

$${
d_1=d_2=d_3=d_4=d_5=d_6
}$$

Unlike square grids, no directional bias exists.

This property dramatically improves pathfinding behavior.

---

# 4.7 Approximation of Circular Coverage

Drone coverage areas are naturally circular.

A drone flying from a depot with maximum range:

$${
R_{max}
}$$

creates a circular reachable region.

Among all regular polygons capable of tessellating the plane, the hexagon most closely approximates a circle.

This follows from the Isoperimetric Principle.

The circle minimizes perimeter for a given area.

Among tessellating polygons:

* Triangle
* Square
* Hexagon

the hexagon provides the closest approximation.

Therefore:

Hexagonal cells minimize geometric distortion while maintaining exact tessellation.

---

# 4.8 Hexagonal Demand Allocation

Suppose administrative unit:

$${
i
}$$

contains:

$${
m_i
}$$

hexagonal cells.

The normalized demand coefficient:

$${
w_i
}$$

derived in Chapter 2 is distributed among those cells.

For cell:

$${
h
}$$

inside administrative unit:

$${
i
}$$

the local demand becomes:

$${
w_h
=

\frac{w_i}{m_i}

# 4.9 Hexagons as Graph Nodes

The transition from geometry to graph theory occurs naturally.

Each hexagon center becomes a graph vertex.

Formally:

$${
V=
{v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n}
}$$

where:

$${
v_i
}$$

corresponds to the centroid of hexagon:

$${
h_i
}$$
Edges are created between neighboring cells.

Thus:

$${
E=
{(v_i,v_j)}
}$$

whenever two hexagons share a boundary.

The resulting graph is:

$${
G=(V,E)
}$$

which forms the routing substrate of the TiranaFly platform.

# 5.1 Introduction

The hexagonal tessellation framework developed in Chapter 4 transforms the Municipality of Tirana into a collection of discrete spatial cells.

While this representation is suitable for geographic analysis, optimization algorithms require a more abstract mathematical structure capable of representing movement, connectivity, costs, and constraints.

Graph Theory provides such a framework.

A graph is one of the most powerful mathematical tools available for modeling transportation systems, communication networks, supply chains, logistics infrastructures, and autonomous routing systems.

In the context of TiranaFly, the municipality is transformed into a weighted graph where:

- Hexagonal cells become vertices
    
- Flight corridors become edges
    
- Distance, energy, and travel time become edge weights

This graph becomes the mathematical foundation upon which all routing and optimization decisions are made.

---

# 5.2 Fundamentals of Graph Theory

A graph is formally defined as: $G=(V,E)$ where: ${V={v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n}}$ is the set of vertices and
${E={e_1,e_2,\ldots,e_m}}$ is the set of edges. Vertices represent entities.

Edges represent relationships between entities.

For TiranaFly: 
$${V = \text{Hexagonal Cells + Depots + Waypoints}}$$
$${E = \text{Valid Flight Connections}}$$

---

# 5.3 Why Graph Theory Is Suitable for UAV Logistics

A drone logistics system naturally forms a graph structure.

Each delivery location can be represented as a node.

Each feasible flight path can be represented as an edge.

Consequently:

|Real Object|Graph Representation|
|---|---|
|Depot|Vertex|
|Customer Cell|Vertex|
|Waypoint|Vertex|
|Flight Corridor|Edge|
|Flight Distance|Weight|
|Energy Cost|Weight|
|Flight Time|Weight|

The graph abstraction allows the use of:

- Shortest Path Algorithms
    
- Facility Location Models
    
- Vehicle Routing Problems
    
- Flow Optimization Models
    
- Network Resilience Analysis

e_{ij}

# 5.7 Haversine Distance Calculation

Distances cannot be computed using ordinary Euclidean geometry because the Earth is spherical.

Instead, TiranaFly uses the Haversine Formula.

Let: $(\phi_1,\lambda_1)$ and $(\phi_2,\lambda_2)$ be two geographic coordinates. Then:
$${
d=2R\arcsin\left(\sqrt{\sin^2\left(\frac{\phi_2-\phi_1}{2}\right)+\cos(\phi_1)\cos(\phi_2)\sin^2\left(\frac{\lambda_2-\lambda_1}{2}\right)}\right)
}$$
where:

- $(R)$ = Earth radius
    
- $(\phi)$ = latitude
    
- $(\lambda)$ = longitude

The Haversine distance becomes the primary geometric metric used throughout the project.

---

# 5.8 Edge Weight Modeling

Not all flight paths are equally desirable.

Consequently, every edge receives a cost value.

The simplest cost function is: $w_{ij}=d_{ij}$. However, drone logistics requires more sophisticated modeling. Therefore TiranaFly adopts:
$${  
w_{ij}
=
\alpha d_{ij}  
+  
\beta t_{ij}  
+  
\gamma E_{ij}  
}$$
where:

- $(d_{ij})$ = distance
    
- $(t_{ij})$ = travel time
    
- $(E_{ij})$ = battery consumption

and: $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are configurable weighting parameters.

# 5.9 Battery Energy Model

Energy consumption is one of the most important constraints.

Let:

- $(m_d)$ = drone mass
    
- $(m_p)$ = payload mass
    
- $(v)$ = cruising velocity

Then:
$${  
E_{ij}
=
P_{avg}  
\cdot  
\frac{d_{ij}}{v}  
}$$
where: $P_{avg}$ represents average power consumption. This allows the routing engine to optimize for energy rather than merely distance.

# Chapter 6 — Routing and Path Optimization Framework

# 6.1 Introduction

The graph model developed in Chapter 5 provides a mathematical representation of the Municipality of Tirana as a network of interconnected nodes and edges.

However, the existence of a graph alone does not solve the logistics problem.

The fundamental operational question remains:

> Given a depot and a delivery destination, what route should a drone follow?

This question belongs to a class of problems known as **Shortest Path Problems (SPP)**.

Shortest path algorithms are among the most studied topics in Graph Theory, Transportation Science, Artificial Intelligence, and Operations Research.

For TiranaFly, routing decisions directly affect:

* Delivery time
* Battery consumption
* Infrastructure utilization
* Fleet productivity
* Operational costs

Consequently, selecting appropriate routing algorithms is critical for system performance.

# 6.2 The Routing Problem

Let: $G=(V,E,W)$ be the weighted graph defined in Chapter 5.

Suppose: $s \in V$ represents the source depot and $t \in V$ represents the delivery destination.

The objective is to find: $P(s,t)$ such that:
$${Cost(P)=\sum_{e \in P}w(e)}$$
is minimized.

The path: $P^*$ satisfying:$${P^*=\arg\min Cost(P)}$$is called the optimal route.

---

## Minimum Time Routing

Objective: $\min \sum t_{ij}$

Advantages:

* Fast deliveries

Disadvantages:

* May consume more energy

---

## Minimum Energy Routing

Objective: $\min \sum E_{ij}$

Advantages:

* Maximizes battery life

Disadvantages:

w_{ij}

allowing different operational priorities.

# 6.4 Dijkstra's Algorithm

The primary routing algorithm used by TiranaFly is:

**Dijkstra's Shortest Path Algorithm**

Developed by: Edsger W. Dijkstra in 1956.

It computes the shortest path between a source node and every other node in a weighted graph.

---

## Mathematical Principle

Let: $d(v)$ represent the currently known shortest distance from the source. Initially: $d(s)=0$ and $d(v)=\infty$ for all other vertices.

The algorithm repeatedly selects the node with minimum tentative distance and relaxes its outgoing edges.

For edge: $(u,v)$ the update rule becomes:
$${
d(v)
=
\min
\left(
d(v),
d(u)+w(u,v)
\right)
}$$
---

## Computational Complexity

Using a binary heap: $O((V+E)\log V)$. This is sufficiently efficient for city-scale routing.

---

## Why Dijkstra Was Selected

Advantages:

* Guaranteed optimal solution
* Easy implementation
* Works with weighted graphs
* Supported by NetworkX
* Well-understood mathematically

For static routing problems, Dijkstra provides excellent performance.

---

# 6.5 A* Search Algorithm

While Dijkstra explores the graph uniformly, A* introduces heuristic guidance.

The evaluation function is:
$${
f(n)
=
g(n)
+
h(n)
}$$
where:

* $(g(n))$ = accumulated cost
* $(h(n))$ = estimated remaining cost

---

## Heuristic Function

For geographic routing:

$${
h(n)
=
\text{HaversineDistance}(n,t)
}$$

where: $t$

is the target node.

This estimate is admissible because straight-line distance never exceeds actual route length.

---

## Benefits of A*

Compared with Dijkstra:

* Fewer node expansions
* Faster execution
* Better scalability

Especially beneficial for large networks.

---

# 6.6 Comparison of Dijkstra and A*

| Criterion                 | Dijkstra | A*         |
| ------------------------- | -------- | ---------- |
| Optimality                | Yes      | Yes        |
| Uses Heuristic            | No       | Yes        |
| Speed                     | Moderate | Fast       |
| Complexity                | Medium   | Low–Medium |
| Implementation Difficulty | Easy     | Moderate   |

TiranaFly therefore supports both algorithms.

Dijkstra serves as the baseline.

A* serves as the production routing engine.

---

# 6.7 Energy-Aware Routing

Traditional shortest path algorithms minimize distance.

Drone routing requires minimizing energy.

Therefore:

$${
w_{ij}
=
E_{ij}
}$$

can replace distance weights.

This transforms Dijkstra into an energy optimization algorithm.

The resulting path may be longer geographically but cheaper energetically.

---

# 6.8 Battery-Constrained Routing

A route is only feasible if battery capacity permits completion. Let: $B_{max}$ denote battery capacity. The route: $P$ is feasible only if:
$${
\sum_{e \in P}
E_e
\le
B_{max}
}$$
Otherwise: $P$ is rejected. This creates a constrained shortest-path problem.

---

# 6.9 Weather-Aware Routing

Weather significantly affects UAV performance.

Wind modifies:

* Flight time
* Energy consumption
* Safety

Let: $W_{ij}$ represent weather impact. The edge weight becomes:
$${
w_{ij}
=
\alpha d_{ij}
+
\beta E_{ij}
+
\delta W_{ij}
}$$
This allows dynamic route adaptation.

---

# 6.10 No-Fly Zones

Certain airspaces cannot be traversed.

Examples include:

* Government facilities
* Military areas
* Airports
* Restricted infrastructure

Let: $Z$ represent the set of restricted cells. Then: $V_Z \subseteq V$. Any edge intersecting $V_Z$ is removed from the graph. This process is known as: **Graph Pruning**

# 6.11 Route Reconstruction

After shortest path computation, the optimal path must be reconstructed.

Let: $pred(v)$ represent the predecessor of node: $v$. The route is obtained by backtracking:
$${
t
\rightarrow pred(t)
\rightarrow pred(pred(t))
\rightarrow \cdots
\rightarrow s
}$$
This sequence produces the final flight trajectory.

# 7.2 Facility Location Theory

Facility Location Theory is a branch of Operations Research concerned with determining where facilities should be located to optimize system performance.

Applications include:

* Warehouses
* Hospitals
* Fire stations
* Telecommunications towers
* Distribution centers
* Drone depots

Facility location problems seek to answer:

> Which locations minimize total service cost while maintaining adequate coverage?

The TiranaFly depot placement problem belongs to the family of:

### Discrete Facility Location Problems

because depot candidates are selected from a finite set of feasible geographic locations.

---

# 7.3 Depot Placement Objectives

Several competing objectives must be balanced.

---

##### Objective 1 — Minimize Travel Distance

$$\min \sum d_{ij}$$

Where: $d_{ij}$ represents distance between depot $j$ and demand cell $i$

---

##### Objective 2 — Maximize Coverage

Every demand node should be reachable.
$$Coverage \approx 100\%$$
---

##### Objective 3 — Minimize Infrastructure Costs

Building depots is expensive. Let: $f_j$ represent the fixed construction cost of depot $j$. Then:
$$\min \sum f_j$$
---

##### Objective 4 — Balance Workload

Demand should not concentrate excessively at one depot.

---

# 7.7 The p-Median Problem

One of the most widely used facility location models is the p-Median Problem. The objective is to locate $p$ depots minimizing weighted travel distance.

---

## Decision Variables

Depot activation:
$${
y_j=
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if depot } j \text{ is selected}\
\\0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
}$$
Demand assignment:
$${
x_{ij}
=
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if cell } i \text{ assigned to depot } j\
\\0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
}$$
---

## Objective Function

$${
\min
\sum_i
\sum_j
w_i d_{ij} x_{ij}
}$$
This minimizes population-weighted travel distance.

---

## Constraints

+ Each demand node must be assigned $\sum_j x_{ij}=1$ for all $i$.
+ Assignment only to active depots $x_{ij}\le y_j$.
+ Exactly $p$ depots $\sum_j y_j=p$

# 7.8 Set Covering Formulation

Coverage is often more important than distance. For drones every demand cell must lie within: $R_{max}$ of at least one depot.

---

Define:

$${
a_{ij}
=
\begin{cases}
1,& d_{ij}\le R_{max}\
\\0,& otherwise
\end{cases}
}$$
---

Objective: ${\min\sum_j y_j}$

---

Subject to: ${\sum_j a_{ij}y_j\ge 1}$ for all demand cells. This guarantees complete coverage.

---

# 7.9 Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP)

The TiranaFly depot placement model combines:

* p-Median
* Set Covering
* Capacity Constraints

into a single MILP framework.

---

## Objective

$${
\min
\left(
\sum_j f_j y_j
+
\sum_i \sum_j c_{ij}x_{ij}
\right)
}$$

where:

* $(f_j)$ = depot construction cost
* $(c_{ij})$ = assignment cost

---

This simultaneously minimizes:

* CAPEX
* Travel costs

---

# Chapter 8 — Fleet Sizing, Queueing Theory and Capacity Planning

---

# 8.1 Introduction

Determining optimal depot locations alone does not guarantee satisfactory logistics performance.

Even if every customer lies within the coverage radius of a depot, the system can still fail if there are insufficient drones available to serve incoming delivery requests.

The fundamental challenge becomes:

> How many drones should be allocated to each depot?

Allocating too few drones results in:

- Delivery delays
    
- Queue buildup
    
- Customer dissatisfaction
    
- Service instability

Allocating too many drones results in:

- Unnecessary capital expenditures
    
- Increased maintenance costs
    
- Reduced utilization rates

Therefore fleet sizing must be formulated as an optimization problem rather than a subjective engineering estimate.

---

# 8.2 The Capacity Planning Problem

Let $D_j$ represent depot $(j)$. Each depot receives delivery requests arriving randomly through time. Let: $\lambda_j$ denote the average arrival rate of delivery requests at depot $(j)$. Measured in: $\text{requests/hour}$ each drone can process deliveries at rate $\mu$ measured in $\text{deliveries/hour}$. The problem is determining the minimum number of drones $c_j$ required at depot $(j)$ such that service remains stable.

---

# 8.3 Why Queueing Theory Is Needed

Delivery requests do not arrive at regular intervals. Real-world demand behaves stochastically.

Examples:

- Morning demand spike
    
- Lunch rush
    
- Evening peak
    
- Weekend variation
    
- Holiday surges

Thus: $Arrival\ Times$ must be modeled as random variables. Queueing theory provides the mathematical framework for analyzing these systems.

---

# 8.4 Stochastic Arrival Process

The standard assumption for logistics systems is a Poisson arrival process.

Let $N(t)$ represent the number of requests arriving during interval (t). Then: $N(t)  \sim Poisson(\lambda t)$ with probability mass function:  
$$P(N(t)=k)=\frac{(\lambda t)^k e^{-\lambda t}}{k!}$$
---

## Why Poisson Is Appropriate

The Poisson model is suitable because:

- Requests occur independently
    
- Demand is unpredictable
    
- Large customer populations exist
    
- Widely validated in logistics systems

Consequently: $Interarrival\ Times$ follow an exponential distribution.

---

# 8.5 Exponential Interarrival Times

If arrivals are Poisson $T \sim Exp(\lambda)$ with density $f(t)= \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ and expected value $E[T]= \frac{1}{\lambda}$.  This provides the average time between requests.

---

# 8.6 Service Process

Each drone performs:

1. Package loading
    
2. Takeoff
    
3. Flight
    
4. Delivery
    
5. Return
    
6. Battery swap

The complete cycle requires $T_s$ minutes. Therefore $\mu = \frac{60}{T_s}$ deliveries per hour.

---

## Example

Suppose $T_s=20$ minutes. Then $\mu = 3$ deliveries/hour. Each drone can perform approximately $3$ deliveries per hour.

---

# 8.7 M/M/c Queue Model

The TiranaFly depot is modeled as $M/M/c$ queue.

Meaning:

| Symbol     | Meaning             |
| ---------- | ------------------- |
| First $M$  | Poisson arrivals    |
| Second $M$ | Exponential service |
| $c$        | Number of drones    |

This is the standard model used in:

- Airports
    
- Call centers
    
- Hospitals
    
- Logistics hubs

---

# 8.8 Traffic Intensity

The most important queueing metric is utilization. Define: $\rho = \frac{\lambda}{c\mu}$ where:

- $(\lambda)$ = arrival rate
    
- $(\mu)$ = service rate
    
- $(c)$ = drones

---

## Interpretation

If $\rho < 1$ the system is stable.

If $\rho > 1$ queues grow indefinitely.

---

## Stability Condition

Therefore $c = \frac{\lambda}{\mu}$ must always hold. This represents the minimum fleet requirement.

---

# 8.9 Depot Demand Estimation

Demand derives directly from population.

From Chapter 2:
$${w_i
=
\frac{P_i}{807029}  
}$$

Suppose: $\Lambda$ represents total municipal demand. Then depot demand becomes:
$${
\lambda_j
=
w_j\Lambda  
}$$
Thus larger populations generate more delivery requests.

# 8.10 Example Fleet Calculation

Municipality demand: $\Lambda=600$ deliveries/hour. Suppose Depot A serves 45% of demand.
Then: ${\lambda_A=270}$ requests/hour.

Assume: $\mu=3$ deliveries/hour.

Then: ${c_A=\frac{270}{3}90}$ drones.

Therefore: $c_A=91$ is the minimum stable fleet.

# 8.11 Expected Queue Length

For an M/M/c system:

Expected queue length $L_q$ measures waiting requests. Higher values indicate congestion. Lower values indicate efficient operations. Minimizing $L_q$ is a primary objective.

# 8.12 Average Waiting Time

Using Little's Law: $L=\lambda W$ where:

- $(L)$ = average customers in system
    
- $(W)$ = average waiting time

Therefore: ${W=\frac{L}{\lambda}}$

---

## Operational Interpretation

A depot with $W=2$ minutes performs well. A depot with $W=30$ minutes requires additional drones.

# Chapter 9 — UAV Energy Consumption, Battery Modeling and Flight Feasibility

---

# 9.1 Introduction

A logistics network can only function if its vehicles possess sufficient energy to complete assigned tasks.

Unlike ground vehicles that can refuel quickly, UAVs operate under severe battery limitations.

Battery capacity directly determines:

- Maximum flight range
    
- Payload capability
    
- Route feasibility
    
- Fleet productivity
    
- Operating costs

Therefore energy modeling becomes one of the most important constraints in the TiranaFly optimization framework.

The objective of this chapter is to construct a realistic mathematical model of drone energy consumption and integrate it into routing and facility planning decisions.

---

# 9.2 Energy as a Constrained Resource

Every delivery mission consumes energy.

For a flight: $F=(v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n)$ the total energy required is: $E_{total}=\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}  E_i$ where $E_i$
represents energy consumed along segment $(v_i,v_{i+1})$. A mission is feasible only if:
$${
E_{total}  
\le  
B_{usable}}$$
where $B_{usable}$ represents usable battery capacity.

---

The TiranaFly fleet is modeled after a medium-capacity logistics multirotor.

Assumed specifications:

|Parameter|Value|
|---|---|
|Empty Mass|9.5 kg|
|Payload Capacity|5.0 kg|
|Cruise Speed|15 m/s|
|Battery Capacity|1200 Wh|
|Battery Safety Reserve|20%|
|Maximum Operational Range|24 km|

Only 80% of battery capacity is considered usable. Thus
$${B_{usable}
=
 0.8  
\times  
1200
=
960Wh  
}$$
---

# 9.4 Energy Consumption Components

Drone power consumption originates from multiple sources.

Largest contributor.

## Environmental Effects

Includes:

E_{total}

E_{propulsion}

E_{avionics}

+  
E_{environment}  
}$$

# 9.5 Flight Time Model

Let d represent route distance. Let v represent average speed. Flight duration becomes:   
$${t
=
\frac{d}{v}  
}$$

---
## Example

Distance: $d=12km$ 

Speed: $v=15m/s$  

Converting: $15m/s=54km/h$

Therefore $t=\frac{12}{54} = 0.222h$ or $13.3min$ 

---

# 9.6 Payload Impact Model

Payload mass significantly affects energy requirements. Let $m_e$ represent empty drone mass. Let $m_p$ represent payload mass.
Total mass becomes: $m=m_e+m_p$. The TiranaFly model assumes: $E  \propto m$. Therefore $E=kmd$ where $k$ is an experimentally determined coefficient. This linear approximation is commonly used in UAV logistics studies.

---

# 9.7 Aerodynamic Energy Model

A more detailed model incorporates flight duration.
$$E=P \times t$$

where $P$ represents average power draw. Substituting:$$t=\frac{d}{v}$$gives$$ 
E
=
P  
\frac{d}{v}  
$$

This establishes direct dependence on:

- Distance
    
- Velocity
    
- Power demand

---

# 9.8 TiranaFly Operational Energy Equation

Combining payload and power effects:$$  
E_{ij}
=
\left[  
(m_e+m_p)  
g  
\frac{1}{\eta}  
\frac{d_{ij}}{v}  
\right]  
+  
\epsilon  
\frac{d_{ij}}{v}  
$$
where:

| Symbol     | Meaning               |
| ---------- | --------------------- |
| (m_e)      | Empty mass            |
| (m_p)      | Payload               |
| (g)        | Gravity               |
| (\eta)     | Propulsion efficiency |
| (d_{ij})   | Route distance        |
| (v)        | Velocity              |
| (\epsilon) | Avionics power        |

This equation is used throughout the optimization framework.

---

# 9.9 Battery Safety Constraints

Professional UAV systems never allow full battery depletion.

A safety reserve is mandatory.

Let $R$ denote reserve percentage. TiranaFly uses: $R$=20%. Mission feasibility requires:
$$  
E_{mission}  
\le  
(1-R)B
$$
This prevents emergency battery failures.

A delivery mission requires:

1. Outbound flight
    
2. Delivery
    
3. Return flight

Therefore:$$
E_{round}
=
2E_{outbound}$$
must satisfy:
$$
E_{round}  
\le  
B_{usable}  
$$
This condition directly determines maximum delivery radius.

# 9.11 Maximum Range Estimation

Let $e$ represent energy consumed per kilometer. Maximum safe range becomes
$$
R_{max}
=
\frac{B_{usable}}  
{2e}  $$

The factor 2 accounts for return travel.

---

The Municipality of Tirana exhibits significant elevation variation.

Regions such as:

- Krrabë
    
- Dajt
    
- Zall Bastar

contain mountainous terrain. Climbing requires additional energy.

Let $\Delta h$ represent altitude gain. Potential energy required is $E_h=mg\Delta h$
The total route energy becomes $E=E_{horizontal}+E_h$

# 9.13 Wind Effects

Wind strongly influences drone performance. Tailwinds reduce energy. Headwinds increase energy. Let $v_w$ represent wind speed. Effective velocity becomes $v_{eff}=v-v_w$. The energy equation becomes $E=P\frac{d}{v_{eff}}$ showing increased consumption under adverse weather.

---

# 9.14 Temperature Effects

Lithium batteries lose performance in cold conditions.

Empirical studies show:

| Temperature | Capacity Reduction |
| ----------- | ------------------ |
| 20°C        | 0%                 |
| 10°C        | 5%                 |
| 0°C         | 15%                |
| -10°C       | 25%                |

Therefore $B_{usable}$ must be adjusted seasonally.

---

# 9.15 Energy-Aware Routing

The routing chapter minimized: $w_{ij}$ Edge weights can now be defined as $w_{ij}=E_{ij}$ instead of distance. Consequently Dijkstra and A* become energy optimization algorithms. The resulting route minimizes battery consumption rather than geometric length.

Advantages:

Any infeasible route is removed from consideration.

Among these, the regular hexagon provides the most circle-like structure.

## B.2.2 Hexagon Area Derivation

The perimeter is $P_h=6s$

## B.2.4 Compactness Ratio

The compactness ratio is $C=\frac{4\pi A}{P^2}$. For a regular hexagon: $C=0.907$. For a square: $C=0.785$
Thus hexagons are significantly closer to circles.

# B.7 A* Algorithm Derivation

A* extends Dijkstra using a heuristic.

---

Define: $f(n)=g(n)+h(n)$ where $g(n) =actual\ cost$ and $h(n)=estimated\ remaining\ cost$

For geographic routing: $h(n)=Haversine(n,goal)$

## Admissibility

A heuristic is admissible if:$h(n)\le h^*(n)$

Since straight-line distance never exceeds the shortest route distance $h(n)$ is admissible. Therefore A* remains optimal.

Distribute administrative demand into local hexagonal cells.

---

## Mathematical Formulation

$$\Lambda_i  
\frac{\rho_h}  
{\sum_k \rho_k}  
$$

where:

$$  
\rho_h  
$$

represents local density.

---

## Pseudocode

```
For each Administrative Unit

    Calculate Total Density

    For each Hexagon

        Demand =
        UnitDemand *
        LocalDensity /
        TotalDensity
```

---

## Complexity

$$  
O(n)  
$$

---

## Benefits

- Preserves population totals
- Creates realistic local demand
- Enables high-resolution analysis

---

# D.6 Algorithm 4 — Population Weighted K-Means

## Objective

Find optimal depot locations.

---

## Mathematical Objective

$$  
J=  
\sum_i  
w_i  
|x_i-\mu|^2  
$$

---

## Input

Hexagonal demand cells.

---

## Output

Depot locations.

---

## Pseudocode

```
Choose K Initial Centroids

Repeat

    Assign each point
    to nearest centroid

    Compute weighted centroid

    Update centroid

Until convergence
```

---

## Complexity

$$  
O(nkt)  
$$

where:

- (n) = cells
- (k) = clusters
- (t) = iterations

---

## Convergence

The algorithm terminates when:

$$  
\Delta J \approx 0  
$$

or a maximum iteration limit is reached.

---

# D.7 Why K-Means Was Chosen

Alternative methods considered:

|Method|Rejected Because|
|---|---|
|Random Placement|Non-optimal|
|Hierarchical Clustering|Computationally expensive|
|DBSCAN|Density dependent|
|Spectral Clustering|Computationally heavy|

---

## Advantages of K-Means

- Fast
- Simple
- Robust
- Widely used
- Easy integration

---

## Limitation

Optimizes Euclidean distance rather than transportation cost.

---

# D.8 Algorithm 5 — p-Median Optimization

## Objective

Minimize total weighted travel distance.

---

## Mathematical Formulation

$$  
\min  
\sum_i  
\sum_j  
d_{ij}  
x_{ij}  
$$

---

## Constraints

### Assignment Constraint

Each demand node must be assigned exactly once.

$$  
\sum_j x_{ij}=1  
$$

---

### Facility Count Constraint

Exactly (p) depots must be selected.

$$  
\sum_j y_j=p  
$$

---

## Pseudocode

```
Initialize Candidate Facilities

Build Cost Matrix

Solve MILP

Return Optimal Depot Set
```

---

## Complexity

$$  
NP\text{-Hard}  
$$

---

## Advantage

Unlike K-Means, p-Median directly minimizes transportation cost.

---

# D.9 Why p-Median is Better than K-Means

K-Means optimizes:

```
Euclidean Distance
```

---

p-Median optimizes:

```
Transportation Cost
```

---

## Implication

For real deployment:

- K-Means is suitable for prototyping
- p-Median should replace K-Means in production

# D.12 Algorithm 8 — Haversine Distance

## Purpose

Accurately compute Earth-surface distances.

---

## Mathematical Formula

$$  
d=  
2R  
\arcsin  
\left(  
\sqrt{  
\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta\phi}{2}\right)  
+  
\cos\phi_1  
\cos\phi_2  
\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta\lambda}{2}\right)  
}  
\right)  
$$

---

## Pseudocode

```
Convert Coordinates to Radians

Compute:

Δφ

Δλ

Evaluate Haversine Formula

Return Distance
```

---

## Complexity

$$  
O(1)  
$$

---

## Reason for Selection

Provides sufficient geographic accuracy for municipal-scale routing.

# D.13 Algorithm 9 — Dijkstra Routing

## Purpose

Find minimum-cost route.

---

## Mathematical Problem

Given:

$$  
G=(V,E)  
$$

find the shortest path from:

$$  
s  
$$

to:

$$  
t  
$$

---

## Pseudocode

```
Set Source Distance = 0

Push Source Into Priority Queue

While Queue Not Empty

    Extract Minimum

    Relax Neighbors

Return Path
```

---

## Complexity

$$  
O((V+E)\log V)  
$$

---

## Properties

- Complete
- Optimal
- Deterministic

---

# D.14 Dijkstra Correctness Proof

Dijkstra relies on the:

**Bellman Optimality Principle**

If a path is optimal:

every subpath of that path is also optimal.

Therefore, once a node becomes finalized:

its shortest distance can never improve.

---

## Consequence

The algorithm always produces the globally shortest path for graphs with non-negative edge weights.

---

# D.15 Algorithm 10 — A* Search

## Motivation

Dijkstra explores many unnecessary nodes.

A* introduces a heuristic.

---

## Evaluation Function
$$
g(n)  
+  
h(n)  
$$

where:

- $(g(n))$ = cost from source
- $(h(n))$= heuristic estimate

---

## Heuristic

$$HaversineDistance(n,goal)  
$$

---

## Pseudocode

```
Insert Source

While Open Set Exists

    Select Lowest f(n)
    Expand Node
    Update Costs

Return Path
```

---

## Complexity

Best case:

$$  
O(\log n)  
$$

Practical performance:

Much faster than Dijkstra.

---

## Advantage

Explores fewer nodes while preserving optimality.

# F.11 Why Hexagons?

Three polygons tessellate a plane:

1. Triangle
2. Square
3. Hexagon

Hexagons provide:

* uniform adjacency
* minimal distortion
* isotropic routing

Every hexagon has: $6$ neighbors. Squares have: $4+4$ unequal neighbors. This introduces directional bias.
